## Overview

On October 30th, I had the privilege of attending the **8th E.DSO, EE-ISAC, ENCS, and ENISA Cyber Security Forum**, together with Mattia Hemeryckx.

The forum focused on strengthening the European power grid, improving cyber threat intelligence, and discussing the geopolitical and legislative challenges surrounding critical infrastructure security in Europe.

One thing became very clear throughout the event: securing Europe’s energy ecosystem is no longer only a technical challenge. It is also a challenge of trust, collaboration, resilience, and policy.

## Solar Is Becoming Critical Infrastructure

One of the most interesting discussions focused on the rapid growth of solar energy infrastructure across Europe.

With millions of solar rooftops already deployed and significant growth still ahead, consumers are gradually becoming energy producers themselves. This changes the traditional structure of the grid and introduces entirely new cybersecurity considerations.

As energy systems move closer to homes and businesses, securing the grid also becomes more decentralized and complex.

![European energy grid discussion](/images/blog/enisa-8th-edition/event-photo-03.jpeg)

## Smart Grid, Smart Security

The forum also highlighted how technologies such as EV chargers, grid-connected batteries, and large-scale data centers bring both opportunities and risks.

While these technologies create flexibility and efficiency within the energy ecosystem, they also expand the attack surface. Topics such as instability risks, metering fraud, and vulnerabilities within connected infrastructure were recurring themes throughout the discussions.

This reinforced how important it is to design security measures that are not only strong, but also scalable and adaptable for modern smart grids.

## Information Sharing Is Essential

Another major takeaway was the importance of information sharing and cross-sector collaboration.

Cybersecurity cannot be approached in isolation, especially when protecting infrastructure that crosses borders and connects multiple sectors. Many speakers emphasized that Europe’s cyber resilience depends heavily on cooperation between organizations, governments, operators, and security communities.

Defending in silos simply does not work efficiently against modern threats.

## The Modbus Vulnerability Demo

One of the highlights of the forum was a live demonstration showcasing vulnerabilities within the Modbus protocol.

The session demonstrated how legacy industrial protocols can still be exploited today and why innovation, regulation, and secure-by-design approaches need to evolve together.

It was a strong reminder that many critical infrastructures still rely on technologies that were never originally designed with modern cybersecurity threats in mind.

![Modbus vulnerability demonstration](/images/blog/enisa-8th-edition/event-photo-01.jpeg)

## Final Thoughts

This forum reinforced that cybersecurity within critical infrastructure extends far beyond technology alone.

Trust, coordination, legislation, governance, and consistency are just as important as technical defenses. With the threat landscape evolving rapidly, adaptable policies and strong collaboration will be critical for protecting Europe’s future energy infrastructure.

A big thank you to all speakers and organizers for sharing both technical and strategic insights throughout the event.
