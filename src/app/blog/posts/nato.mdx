## Overview

To close off the lecture period, I attended another insightful **Tech&Meet** session organized by **HOWEST University of Applied Sciences**.

This session was led by **Martin de Pauw** from the **NATO Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence (CCDCOE)**, who shared valuable insights into how NATO’s cyber defence operations evolved into their current form.

Rather than focusing purely on technical depth, the session emphasized something much more fundamental: the importance of **people, processes, and technology** working together effectively.

## Cybersecurity at Scale

One of the strongest takeaways from the session was how cybersecurity at a large scale is not primarily about chasing every possible threat.

Instead, mature cyber defence relies heavily on:

- Structured decision-making
- Risk awareness
- Coordination
- Governance
- Operational maturity

This perspective resonated strongly with me because it aligns closely with my own interest in **Cyber Risk Management** and GRC.

![Cyber defence strategy discussion](/images/blog/nato-t&m/event-photo-01.jpg)

## Risk Cannot Always Be Eliminated

Another important insight from the session was the idea that cybersecurity is not about eliminating all risks entirely.

In practice, organizations — especially large institutions such as NATO — need to continuously assess, prioritize, and manage risk in a structured and realistic way.

That mindset applies not only to cyber defence, but increasingly to areas such as artificial intelligence and emerging technologies as well.

Maturity comes less from novelty and more from:

- Good judgement
- Clear processes
- Consistency
- Adaptability
- Communication

## Why This Perspective Matters

As cybersecurity evolves, technical tooling alone is no longer enough.

Strong governance, collaboration, and strategic awareness are becoming just as important as technical capabilities themselves. Sessions like this reinforce how cybersecurity professionals increasingly need to understand both the technical and organizational sides of security.

For me personally, it was valuable to see how concepts such as governance, risk management, and operational structure play a central role even within highly advanced cyber defence environments.

![NATO cyber defence concepts](/images/blog/nato-t&m/event-photo-02.jpg)

## Final Thoughts

Ending the lecture period with sessions like this was a strong reminder of why these conversations matter.

Cybersecurity is ultimately not only about technology — it is about making informed decisions in constantly evolving environments.

A big thank you to Martin de Pauw and HOWEST for organizing and sharing these insights into cyber defence, operational maturity, and risk-focused thinking.
